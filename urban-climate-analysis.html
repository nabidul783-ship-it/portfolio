Project Summary

An urban climate analysis was completed for the Forest Hills/Kew Gardens area to connect neighborhood layout, land cover, traffic exposure, and environmental behavior. The study examined how vegetation, buildings, concrete, asphalt, and nearby high-traffic roads affected temperature, humidity, air quality, and heat transfer patterns.

The analysis combined land-cover classification, Local Climate Zone evaluation, spatial temperature and humidity mapping, traffic-volume comparison, particulate matter review, CO₂/ozone discussion, roof/surface temperature comparison, and heat-flux evaluation.

The area was divided into multiple Local Climate Zones because the walking route included open low-rise housing, compact low-rise blocks, and a large park region. This helped explain why environmental conditions changed across the route rather than behaving like one uniform neighborhood.

Technical Process

  • Classified land cover into roads, concrete, rooftops/buildings, and vegetation using mapped imagery.
  • Estimated average building height and assigned Local Climate Zones for different portions of the route.
  • Mapped PM temperature and humidity variation along the route.
  • Compared nearby traffic corridors, including Grand Central Parkway, Queens Blvd, and Jackie Robinson Parkway.
  • Reviewed roof temperature, asphalt/surface temperature, sensible heat flux, latent heat flux, and daily storage trends.

Results and Outcomes

  • Estimated land cover at approximately 12% roads, 22% concrete, 28% rooftops/buildings, and 38% vegetation.
  • Estimated average building height at approximately 10 m.
  • Classified portions of the area as LCZ 6 Open Low-Rise, LCZ 3 Compact Low-Rise, and LCZ A for the large park area.
  • Documented nearby daily traffic volumes of approximately 333,000 cars on Grand Central Parkway, 85,000 cars on Queens Blvd, and 70,000 cars on Jackie Robinson Parkway.
  • Found peak roof temperature near 35°C, asphalt/surface temperature near 44°C, sensible heat flux near 196 W/m², latent heat flux near 406 W/m², and daily storage near 29 W/m².
