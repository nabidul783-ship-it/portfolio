6-DOF Robotic Arm Analysis

A kinematic, dynamic, trajectory, and control study of an anthropomorphic arm with a spherical wrist for a reach, grasp, and pick-up task.

Project Summary

A 6-degree-of-freedom anthropomorphic robotic arm with a spherical wrist was analyzed for a reach, grasp, and pick-up task. The robot was treated as an industrial-style revolute-joint manipulator with a gripper, requiring both precise position control and controlled orientation of the end effector.

The analysis covered Denavit-Hartenberg frame assignment, forward kinematics, inverse kinematics, Jacobian assembly, inverse velocity, inverse acceleration, trajectory generation, dynamic torque estimation, and closed-loop control. The robot moved from a home configuration toward an end-effector target position of x = 1 m, y = 0.3 m, and z = 0.15 m.

Because the robot used a spherical wrist, inverse kinematics was split into a position problem for joints 1–3 and an orientation problem for joints 4–6. A damped least-squares style approach was used during trajectory generation to avoid issues near singular configurations.

Technical Process

  • Created DH frames and transformation matrices for all six revolute joints.
  • Derived the end-effector transformation matrix and separated wrist-center position from tool orientation.
  • Built the 6×6 Jacobian using linear and angular velocity contributions from each joint frame.
  • Verified direct Jacobian inversion at the target pose by checking that the determinant was nonzero, with a value of 0.1559.
  • Generated a connected four-piece trajectory and smoothed joint motion using MATLAB quintic polynomial trajectories.
  • Compared PD and PID controllers by tracking joint position, velocity, torque demand, and final end-effector error.

Results and Outcomes

  • Produced smooth joint position, velocity, and acceleration profiles for a multi-segment reach, grasp, and lift motion.
  • Confirmed that the target pose was not singular based on a nonzero Jacobian determinant.
  • Identified gravity as the main contributor to torque, especially at joints 2 and 3, while Coriolis and damping terms were relatively small for the slow motion profile.
  • Found that both PD and PID controllers completed the task, with PID giving lower tracking error and smaller final end-effector error while staying within the assumed torque limits.
